Mood Swings: Three Neuromodulatory Scalars for Impulse–Caution Control in Actor–Critic Agents

Abstract. Neuromodulators in biological brains act as low-bandwidth broadcast signals that reshape learning and behavior. This project tests a minimal analogue for deep reinforcement learning: three global scalars applied to a standard actor–critic update. A dopaminergic gain $k_{\mathrm{DA}}$ scales the temporal-difference (TD) error. Two serotonergic coefficients act on orthogonal channels: an entropy weight $k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}$ and a risk-shaping weight $k^{\mathrm{risk}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}$ that subtracts a differentiable danger potential from reward. The parameters $\mathbf{k}=(k_{\mathrm{DA}}, k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}, k^{\mathrm{risk}}_{\mathrm{5HT}})$ define a compact control surface for trading reward against hazard exposure. In Pac-Mind and MiniHack HazardRooms, sweeping $\mathbf{k}$ yields a smooth safety–performance frontier: higher $k_{\mathrm{DA}}$ accelerates learning but raises collision rates, while higher serotonergic weights increase survival at the cost of slower reward accumulation.

1. Scope and claims

This work is deliberately small in mechanism. The policy and value networks are conventional. The only additional degrees of freedom are three scalars that gate existing learning signals. The empirical claim is narrow: across two hazard-heavy grid domains, these scalars parameterize a family of agents with distinct impulse–caution profiles.

A practical interpretation is online adaptation under continued learning. If learning is turned off, scaling the TD error has no effect, since it only multiplies gradients. The runtime control knob is therefore meaningful when the agent is still updating parameters, or when the system maintains multiple checkpoints trained at different $\mathbf{k}$ values.

2. Preliminaries

We use a Markov decision process $\mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma)$. At time $t$, the agent observes $S_t$, samples $A_t\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid S_t)$, receives reward $R_{t+1}=r(S_t,A_t,S_{t+1})$, and transitions to $S_{t+1}\sim P(\cdot\mid S_t,A_t)$. The discounted return is $G_t=\sum_{k\ge 0}\gamma^k R_{t+k+1}$.

The actor–critic baseline uses a value function $V_w$ and TD error $$\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V_w(S_{t+1}) - V_w(S_t).$$ The critic minimizes $\tfrac12\delta_t^2$ and the actor ascends the policy-gradient surrogate $\log\pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)\,\delta_t$.

3. Neuromodulatory actor–critic

3.1 Three scalars

Neuromodulation is introduced by replacing the baseline learning signal with three scalar gates:

$$\mathbf{k} = (k_{\mathrm{DA}},\; k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}},\; k^{\mathrm{risk}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}).$$

The three components have distinct roles. Risk shaping modifies the reward used in the TD error. Dopamine gain scales the TD error after reward shaping. Entropy drive adds an explicit entropy gradient term to the actor update.

3.2 Risk shaping

Let $\rho(s)$ be a differentiable danger potential. In grid tasks this can be a smooth function of distance to hazards, spikes, lava, or enemies. The shaped reward is

$$R'_{t+1} = R_{t+1} - k^{\mathrm{risk}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}\,\rho(S_{t+1}).$$

The TD error becomes $$\delta_t = R'_{t+1} + \gamma V_w(S_{t+1}) - V_w(S_t).$$ This creates value depressions around hazards, which encourages the actor to route trajectories around dangerous regions.

3.3 Dopaminergic gain

The dopamine gain scales the TD error used in both the actor and critic updates:

$$\delta'_t = k_{\mathrm{DA}}\,\delta_t.$$

In an on-policy actor–critic, scaling $\delta_t$ changes the effective step size of both updates. Larger $k_{\mathrm{DA}}$ can accelerate learning early, but also amplifies gradient noise and can increase instability.

3.4 Serotonergic entropy drive

The actor update includes an entropy term with coefficient $k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}$. Let $H_t=-\sum_a \pi_\theta(a\mid S_t)\log\pi_\theta(a\mid S_t)$. The parameter update is

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha_a\left(\delta'_t\,\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t) + k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}\,\nabla_\theta H_t\right).$$

Increasing $k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}$ broadens the action distribution and increases exploration. This typically increases survival under hazards, but slows exploitation.

3.5 Compact update procedure

Inputs: (S_t, A_t, R_{t+1}, S_{t+1}), danger potential ρ(·), scalars k
1) R' = R_{t+1} - k_5HT^risk * ρ(S_{t+1})
2) δ  = R' + γ V_w(S_{t+1}) - V_w(S_t)
3) δ' = k_DA * δ
4) Critic:  w ← w + α_c δ' ∇_w V_w(S_t)
5) Entropy: H_t = -Σ_a π_θ(a|S_t) log π_θ(a|S_t)
6) Actor:   θ ← θ + α_a [ δ' ∇_θ log π_θ(A_t|S_t) + k_5HT^ent ∇_θ H_t ]

4. Domains

4.1 Pac-Mind

Pac-Mind is a 20×20 toroidal maze. Cells contain walls, pellets (+1), power pellets (+10 and 40 steps of vulnerability), an agent, and four ghosts. Capturing a vulnerable ghost yields +50. Contact with a non-vulnerable ghost ends the episode with −100. Ghosts pursue the agent with probability 0.8 and otherwise move randomly. Episodes terminate on collision, after 3000 steps, or when all pellets are collected.

4.2 MiniHack HazardRooms

HazardRooms are 17×17 ASCII layouts with lava (instant death, −100), floor spikes (−10), and an amulet goal (+100). Each episode samples one of eight room templates, which forces the agent to recompute a safe path. Observations are egocentric tensors that are down-projected to the same 6-channel format used in Pac-Mind so the network is unchanged across tasks.

5. Network and training

5.1 Perception and policy

At each step the agent receives an 11×11×6 egocentric view centered on its position. Two 3×3 convolutional layers (strides 1, 32 then 64 filters, ReLU) produce a 256-dimensional embedding. A linear value head outputs $V_w(S_t)$ and a linear policy head outputs a distribution over four cardinal actions.

5.3 Mood sweep

The sweep spans 36 settings: $k_{\mathrm{DA}}\in\{0.5,1,2,4\}$, $k^{\mathrm{ent}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}\in\{0,0.02,0.05\}$, and $k^{\mathrm{risk}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}\in\{0,0.2,1\}$. Each $\mathbf{k}$ setting is trained from scratch for 50,000 episodes under five random seeds. Every tenth episode runs in evaluation mode with greedy action selection and no learning.

6. Results

6.1 Learning dynamics in Pac-Mind

Higher dopaminergic gain accelerates early learning and increases return variance. The baseline setting $(1,0,0)$ approaches a plateau around 134. A high-dopamine setting $k_{\mathrm{DA}}=4$ reaches a higher plateau around 155 and overtakes the baseline near episode 11,800. A serotonin-dominant setting $(1,0.05,1)$ learns more slowly and converges to a lower but more stable plateau around 124.

6.2 Safety–performance frontier

Across 36 moods, mean return and catastrophic collision probability form a continuous trade-off curve. High $k_{\mathrm{DA}}$ clusters at higher return and higher collision probability. Higher $k^{\mathrm{risk}}_{\mathrm{5HT}}$ shifts the agent toward lower collision probability with lower return. The reported Spearman correlation between mean return and collision probability is 0.91.

6.3 Behavioral signatures

The serotonin entropy coefficient widens the policy. Reported average action entropy is about 0.86 nats for serotonin-dominant settings, compared to about 0.48 nats for dopamine-dominant settings. The risk coefficient reshapes the value function around hazards by constructing local negative regions from $\rho(s)$. In contrast, dopamine gain primarily rescales values without changing which regions are dangerous.

7. Interpretation

The three scalars act as a small, interpretable control interface. Dopamine gain primarily changes how aggressively the system updates, which affects speed and variance. Risk shaping injects a hazard prior through a potential term $\rho(s)$. Entropy drive regulates the action distribution width. Together these axes are sufficient to trace a useful continuum between fast reward collection and cautious survival in the evaluated tasks.

8. Limitations

The danger potential $\rho(s)$ is hand-crafted. This makes the risk axis interpretable, but it also hard-codes what the agent should treat as dangerous. The tasks are discrete grid domains; results may not transfer to continuous control without revisiting how $\rho(s)$ is defined. The sweeps train separate agents per mood, so a single set of weights is not shown to span the entire frontier without further machinery.

9. Suggested extensions

A direct next step is a meta-controller that sets $\mathbf{k}$ online from observables such as surprise, hazard proximity, or explicit safety constraints. Another step is to learn $\rho(s)$ from data while keeping the scalar interface fixed. Both directions preserve the low-dimensional control interface while reducing manual engineering.
